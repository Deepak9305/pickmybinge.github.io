Why Did "The Boys" Kill the Mayor? Unpacking the Show's First Shocking Twist

Clearing the Mandela Effect: The Boys Didn't Do It

If you just typed "why did the boys kill the mayor" into a search bar — stop. Take a breath. Because you're remembering it wrong, and honestly? You're not alone. The Boys didn't kill the mayor. Homelander did.

After multiple seasons of relentless, blood-soaked chaos, the timelines blur. That's just what this show does to your brain. But the Mayor of Baltimore's death didn't happen in Season 3. It wasn't some gritty move by Billy Butcher. It happened in the very first episode — "The Name of the Game" — and it was the exact scene that made audiences sit up, put their phone down, and realize: oh. This show is different. Homelander is different.

The Real Motive: Corporate Blackmail and Compound V

To understand what got the Mayor killed, you have to understand Vought International — specifically how they respond when someone thinks they can corner them. Early in Season 1, the Mayor (played by Malcolm Barrett) decides to play hardball. He wants a supe assigned to Baltimore, specifically Nubian Prince, but Vought's price tag is ridiculous. So the Mayor does what politicians do: he reaches for leverage.

He lets Madelyn Stillwell know he's aware of Compound V — the illegal, buried-six-feet-deep serum Vought uses to actually create superheroes. He tries to blackmail a multi-billion dollar corporation with a god on its payroll. And if you've watched even one episode of The Boys, you already know: that's not leverage. That's a death wish.

The Brutal Plane Scene That Changed Everything

Stillwell doesn't call lawyers. She doesn't negotiate. She just quietly points Homelander at the problem and steps back.

The Mayor is on his private jet, flying back to Baltimore, probably feeling pretty good about himself — convinced he just out-maneuvered Vought. Then he glances out the window. Homelander is floating there. Just hovering. Looking at him.

No words. No warning. Homelander fires his heat vision and cuts the engines clean in half. The plane drops out of the sky and hits the ocean. The Mayor dies. His young son dies. The flight crew dies. Every single person on that jet, gone — and the world will file it as a tragic aviation accident. That's it. That's the whole thing.

Why This Death Defined the Entire Series

That scene was the hook the show needed — and the one most people didn't see coming. Up to that point, Homelander read as a slightly unnerving Superman riff. After it, the entire premise of the show snapped into focus:

  • No one is untouchable: Public office, political power, none of it means anything when Vought decides you're a loose end.
  • There is no moral line: Homelander killed a child without hesitating. Not as an accident — as an acceptable consequence. That tells you everything about who he is.
  • The stakes for Butcher: This scene made clear what Billy Butcher and Hughie are actually fighting. Not corrupt officials. Not garden-variety bad guys. Invincible, corporate-protected sociopaths who answer to no one.

So next time someone in your group chat swears Butcher took out a politician — you've got the receipts. The Boys are chaotic and morally messy, sure. But dropping a private jet into the Atlantic to bury a pharmaceutical secret? That's not Butcher. That's Homelander on a Tuesday.
