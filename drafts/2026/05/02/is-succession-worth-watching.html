Is Succession Worth Watching? A Fan’s Honest Take

Why Succession Has Everyone Talking

If you’re scrolling through HBO’s catalog and wonder, is Succession worth watching, the short answer is a resounding yes—if you love sharp dialogue, power plays, and a family that feels more like a corporate battlefield than a dinner table. From the opening scene where Logan Roy (Brian Cox) storms into a boardroom with a glass of whiskey, the series sets a tone that mixes ruthless ambition with dark humor. It’s not just another drama about rich people; it’s a masterclass in how wealth can both bind and betray.

The show’s pacing feels deliberate, letting tension simmer until a single line or a silent stare flips the entire power dynamic. That’s why viewers keep coming back for every season‑finale cliffhanger and every “what‑the‑hell‑just‑happened?” moment. Whether you’re a fan of corporate intrigue or just love watching characters try (and fail) to outwit each other, Succession delivers the kind of payoff that makes you binge‑watch an entire season in one weekend.

Characters That Keep You Hooked

The Roy family is a cast of anti‑heroes you can’t help but root for, even when they’re scheming against each other. Kendall Roy (Jeremy Strong) is the bruised heir‑apparent whose public breakdown in the “All the Bells” episode (Season 2, Episode 10) is a raw, unforgettable performance. Shiv Roy (Sarah Snook) brings a razor‑sharp wit, constantly juggling her ambition with loyalty to a father who never truly respects her. Her courtroom‑style showdown with Logan in the “Safe Haven” episode (Season 3, Episode 5) showcases her strategic mind.

Then there’s Roman Roy (Kieran Culkin), the sarcastic younger brother who hides his insecurities behind jokes. His awkward yet endearing attempt to impress a potential investor in “The Disruption” (Season 2, Episode 3) reveals a surprisingly tender side. And let’s not forget Tom Wambsgans (Matthew Macfadyen), whose love‑hate relationship with Greg (Nicholas Braun) adds a comic relief that still feels grounded in the series’ high‑stakes world.

Even the peripheral characters—like the ruthless COO Gerri (J. Coleman) or the ever‑watchful legal eagle Marcia (Hiam Abbass)—have moments that feel crucial to the overall power chess. Their brief appearances often carry the weight of an entire boardroom decision, making every scene feel purposeful.

What the Plot Delivers Episode by Episode

Season 1 starts with a simple premise: Logan Roy announces he’s stepping down, and his children scramble for the throne. The pilot sets the stage, but the real fireworks begin in Episode 3, “Lifeboats,” where Kendall’s secret meetings with a potential rival investor reveal the first cracks in his relationship with his father.

  • Episode 5, “I Went to Market,” gives us a masterclass in corporate sabotage as Shiv tries to outmaneuver her brother’s plan to acquire a media startup.
  • Episode 8, “Practicalities,” lands the iconic scene of Kendall stumbling into a press conference, shouting “I’m sorry,” a moment that defines his tragic arc.
  • Season 2’s finale, “This Is Not a Test,” ends with a shocking car accident that changes the power dynamics forever, leaving viewers questioning who will actually inherit the empire.

Season 3 ramps up the stakes with a hostile takeover attempt that pits the siblings against each other in a legal battle reminiscent of a chess match. The episode “All the Bells” (Season 3, Episode 8) showcases Logan’s brutal decision to cut off a potential ally, reminding us that loyalty is a currency he never really values.

Season 4, the most recent, dives deeper into the fallout from the cruise line scandal. Episode “The Disruption” (Season 4, Episode 2) sees Roman trying to prove himself by pitching a new streaming platform, while Shiv’s political ambitions collide with family expectations. The series continues to blend personal trauma with boardroom drama, making each episode feel like a high‑stakes thriller.

Binge‑Worthy Moments That Define the Series

Beyond the overarching plot, Succession thrives on unforgettable set‑pieces. The infamous “Boar on the Floor” scene from Season 1, Episode 9 is a perfect illustration of how the show mixes absurdity with cruelty. Logan forces his children to crawl on the floor while a literal boar snorts nearby—a moment that’s both hilarious and terrifying.

Another standout is the “Shiv and Tom’s wedding” in Season 2, where the family’s dysfunctional dynamics spill over into a ceremony that feels more like a corporate merger than a celebration. The tension between Tom’s eagerness to please and Shiv’s cold calculation creates a palpable electric charge.

Finally, the final episode of Season 3 leaves us with a cliffhanger that still fuels fan theories: Logan’s sudden decision to name a new CEO while the siblings stare at each other in stunned silence. It’s a perfect example of how Succession balances character development with plot twists, ensuring you’ll hit “next episode” without hesitation.
