Is Succession Worth Watching? Yes. God, Yes. Here's Why.

The Hype Is Real — And It Might Actually Be an Understatement

You're staring at the Max catalog. The poster keeps coming up. You've heard people talk about it at dinner, in comment sections, in that one group chat that only activates when something is genuinely unmissable. And you're still not sure if Succession is worth watching. Let me save you the deliberation: yes. Emphatically, painfully, embarrassingly yes.

It hooks you fast. Logan Roy — played by Brian Cox with this terrifying, magnetic stillness — and his son Kendall nervously rapping Beastie Boys in the back of a town car. That's your first two minutes. And somehow, in those two minutes, you already understand everything about their relationship, about power, about who these people are and what they do to each other. The show never lets up after that.

Succession is pitch-black comedy dressed in a prestige drama suit. It's not really about billionaires being awful — though they are, spectacularly. It's about what happens to a family when power becomes the only language anyone speaks. Generational trauma. Hunger that money cannot feed. Shakespeare, but the boardroom is the stage and everyone is both the villain and the fool.

The pacing is slow and deliberate — and that's the point. It lets the pressure build in silence, in half-finished sentences, across dinner tables and mega-yacht decks, until one perfectly chosen line detonates the whole room. That's why you'll be watching at 2 AM telling yourself just one more episode. Again.

The Roy Family: You'll Hate Them. You'll Pity Them. You Won't Be Able to Look Away.

These are not good people. None of them. And yet — and this is what the show does that almost nothing else can — you'll root for all of them anyway. Simultaneously. Against each other.

  • Kendall Roy (Jeremy Strong): The heir who can never quite get there. He's desperate and wounded in a way that's almost unbearable to watch — you want him to win, you know he probably shouldn't, and then he goes and does something catastrophic that makes you question why you were ever on his side. His Season 2 press conference is one of the greatest single moments in television this decade. Full stop.
  • Shiv Roy (Sarah Snook): Brilliant, cutting, and convinced she's the smartest person in every room she walks into. Sometimes she is. She's constantly trying to play both sides — her political conscience and her appetite for her father's approval — and the collision of those two things slowly dismantles her. Her marriage to Tom is its own disaster movie.
  • Roman Roy (Kieran Culkin): The youngest. The loudest. The one who hides everything behind a punchline. Roman is the funniest character in the show and also, quietly, the most broken. The jokes are armor. You only realize how thin that armor is when the show peels it back.
  • Tom & Greg: Tom Wambsgans (Matthew Macfadyen) and Cousin Greg (Nicholas Braun) — the self-proclaimed "Disgusting Brothers" — are the show's comic soul. Two men desperately trying to matter inside a world that sees them as furniture. Their dynamic is absurd and oddly tender and responsible for some of the best scenes in the entire run.

The side characters are just as sharp. Gerri Kellman (J. Smith-Cameron), perpetually exhausted and somehow still the most competent person in the building. Marcia (Hiam Abbass), Logan's wife, who understands the game better than anyone and plays it quietly. Nobody wastes a scene here. Not one.

The Moments That'll Live Rent-Free in Your Head

There are no dragons. No shootouts. The tension in Succession comes from a glance across a conference table, a pause before someone answers a phone call, an insult so precisely targeted it takes a second to register how much damage it just did. A few highlights — no real spoilers on the ending, I promise:

  • The Season 1 Finale ("Nobody Is Ever Missing"): Shiv and Tom's wedding is gorgeous and awful in equal measure. And then Kendall does something late at night that you don't see coming, that changes him irrevocably, that puts him exactly where his father needs him. The final scene is chilling. You'll sit with it for days.
  • "Boar on the Floor" (Season 2, Episode 3): Logan suspects a traitor. His response is to make his millionaire executives crawl on the floor and oink for sausages. It is simultaneously the funniest and most disturbing thing you will watch this year. The scene shouldn't work. It works perfectly.
  • The Season 3 Finale ("All the Bells Say"): The siblings finally do something together. They unite. They have a plan. It's almost hopeful, for about ten minutes. Then someone tips Logan off — and the slow, agonizing reveal of who it was will leave you genuinely speechless. The kind of quiet that descends when a show just got you, completely.

So. Is It Worth It?

Season 4 — the final one — is a different beast. It's quieter. Heavier. It deals with grief in a way that feels almost too real for a show about fictional billionaires, and the GoJo storyline keeps the power games alive until the very last frame. It's the ending this story deserved. Not comfortable. Not tidy. True.

Yes, Succession is worth watching. It's cynical and funny and devastating and impeccably written and it will make you feel things about people you should have no business caring about. Every Emmy it won, it earned. Pour yourself something strong. Meet the Roys. Try not to become emotionally attached.

You're going to become emotionally attached.
